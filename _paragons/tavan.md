## Biography

Tavan Ruul left Iridonia twice: once in spirit, years before his body followed. The second son of a respected warrior clan, he showed every quality his culture demanded: discipline, endurance, a refusal to yield. What he couldn't show was that he was attracted to men, and on Iridonia that was not a private matter. He was a failure of what Zabrak culture expected of its warriors. The culture that prided itself on enduring anything had no patience for that particular truth.

When the Empire formalized its occupation of Iridonian trade corridors and began conscripting able-bodied Zabraks into auxiliary labor roles, Tavan signed onto a Duros freight operation as security. The Duros didn't ask personal questions. That suited him fine. It was on a refueling stop at a backwater station on the Outer Rim that he first encountered a Lasat who went by McCoy. McCoy was quiet, watchful, and clearly using the freight lanes for purposes that had nothing to do with freight. Tavan didn't press. McCoy didn't volunteer. They shared a drink, went separate ways, and kept finding each other anyway. By the third time, they stopped pretending it was coincidence. By the sixth, Tavan had stopped pretending about a great many things.

Tavan spent four years in independent freight before the Empire made neutrality untenable. The Duros-captained hauler called the Merev Daan was interdicted, boarded, and impounded on a falsified contraband charge that was really about the cargo manifest showing non-human ownership. The captain was taken for processing. Tavan wasn't, because an Imperial lieutenant decided a Zabrak with combat credentials was worth recruiting rather than detaining.

Tavan spent eleven days seducing the lieutenant while using his stolen credentials to locate the Merev Daan’s remaining crew. When the lieutenant was reassigned to a Gozanti-class assault carrier, Tavan followed. Before the ship was fully supplied, Tavan hijacked the ship, freed the Merev Daan's surviving crew, shot the Imperial Lieutenant with his own DT-29 heavy blaster pistol, and sent a message to the only person he trusted who operated outside legitimate channels. McCoy arrived with a Partisan flight crew. McCoy’s connection to Saw Gerrera's network ran deeper than he'd admitted, and the Partisans had uses for a Zabrak who could walk into an Imperial facility and walk back out. After their rendezvous, they rechristened the Gozanti as the Inevitable Gravity.

Tavan spent eight months operating on the edges of the Partisans, running interdiction and recovery work that the Alliance wouldn't officially sanction. Tavan and McCoy grew increasingly uncomfortable with Saw's willingness to spend lives for symbolic victories, which culminated when Saw attempted to commandeer a shipment of T-7 disruptor rifles rather than destroy them. They argued about it in the way people argue when they share a bunk and strong opinions. Tavan saw the weapons as a path to justice against the Empire. McCoy had survived the horror the weapons had inflicted in the purge of Lasan. Tavan looked at McCoy’s face and understood for the first time that he'd been confusing justice with revenge. He knew then that Saw had never known the difference. In the end love won over violence.

Tavan broke from the Partisans and approached the Alliance directly, and McCoy came with him. The Alliance accepted Tavan: he brought a captured Gozanti, a battle-hardened crew with stolen chain codes good in a half-dozen Imperial sectors, and one of the few surviving Lasat with Partisan intelligence contacts who could still navigate Saw’s increasingly violent operations. His company assembled the same way his life had: Duros who remembered the Merev Daan, Wookiee warriors who respected the Zabrak culture and the Lasat’s efforts to survive an Imperial purge, and Mon Calamari who wanted something more aggressive than the Alliance but weren’t ready to sacrifice themselves on Saw’s altar of bloodshed. A handful of human commandos attached to the unit are there by choice and have generally stopped noticing species within the first week.

The crew knows about Tavan and McCoy because Tavan never hid it. Tavan had spent enough of his life doing that. McCoy operates nominally as a Rebel agent attached to the unit, which is technically accurate and obscures almost everything relevant about what he actually does. He has stood over too many Partisan graves to romanticize the kind of commander who leads from the front on instinct and fury. McCoy watched Saw spend lives like currency and call it conviction. Tavan is not Saw, and McCoy knows the difference, but he also knows that reckless passion and righteous cause are not the same thing, and that the gap between them is where good people get buried. McCoy stays two steps back, keeps the count, watches the angles Tavan doesn't, and says the quiet word at the right moment that turns a charge into a victory instead of a eulogy. It is the most important thing he does. Tavan has never said so directly; he doesn't need to.
