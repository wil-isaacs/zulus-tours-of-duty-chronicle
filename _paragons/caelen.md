## Biography

Lieutenant Caelen Voss of the Imperial Security Bureau had expected another routine suppression. Palmo, a smoke-choked manufacturing world on the edge of a minor trade corridor, had erupted into riot after weeks of shortages and rumors of rebel agitators. Voss rode in the troop bay of a LAAT/le Patrol Transport with twelve stormtroopers and a pilot detail, reviewing crowd-control directives on his datapad while the gunship knifed through the ash-heavy evening sky. The mission was simple: land near the central plaza, isolate the ringleaders, restore order by force, and remind the population that the Empire’s reach was immediate and absolute.

The first sign of disaster was silence. The engines did not fail; instead, their scream vanished, swallowed by a sudden pressure that made every trooper clutch at helmet seals and chest plates. Outside the viewports, the clouds above Palmo twisted inward, folding into a sphere of black light shot through with blue-white veins. The pilot shouted something Voss never heard. The singularity struck the transport like a fist. Metal shrieked, the deck lurched, and the world became a whirl of fractured stars, torn voices, and impossible reflections—Coruscant skylanes, desert suns, burning walkers, and fleets he could not name flashing in the dark between heartbeats.
When Voss woke, the LAAT/le sat hard-set in violet sand beneath a bruised sky split by jagged auroras, its landing struts half-sunk and one engine nacelle venting smoke from the emergency descent. The air smelled of ozone and burned tibanna. Around him, his troopers unstrapped themselves and spilled from the troop bay, shaken but alive, raising rifles toward a horizon lit by blaster fire. Discipline returned by instinct. Medics checked the injured. The pilot reported the navicomputer was dead, the long-range comm reduced to static and bursts of coded noise from no Imperial channel Voss recognized. Then Corporal Den marked movement below a shattered ridge and magnified it on his macrobinoculars.

Imperials were fighting there—without question, Imperial. Voss saw the clean white lines of stormtroopers advancing beside darker-armored shoretroopers caked in red dust, while a squad of snowtroopers moved in eerie precision across a frost-rimed hollow that should not have existed in that desert waste. Nearby, scout troopers advanced on foot through drifting smoke, their lighter armor and visored helmets ghostly against the violet sand as they swept the ridgeline with compact carbines. The sight was impossible not because the units were Imperial, but because they did not belong together. Their armor, insignia, and machines came from different sectors, different campaigns—perhaps even different years. Yet all of them had the same look of confusion, the same desperate purpose. They too had fallen through the singularity.

The enemy closing around them was no rebel militia. Through drifting smoke Voss glimpsed silhouettes in mismatched armor, banners that dissolved when he tried to focus on them, droids of obsolete design fighting beside soldiers carrying weapons from no catalog he had studied. It was as if whole fragments of war had been torn from the galaxy and dropped into this wasteland together. For one long moment, Lieutenant Caelen Voss understood that rank, doctrine, and certainty had all become fragile things. Palmo was gone. His orders were gone. The Empire itself, once a structure as fixed as durasteel, had become a collection of stranded survivors under a broken sky.

Voss holstered his datapad, drew his service pistol, and opened a command frequency on every band still functioning. “All Imperial elements, this is Lieutenant Voss,” he said, forcing calm into his voice as blaster light flashed across the dunes. “Identify and converge on my position. We establish a perimeter, secure the wounded, and determine where—when—we are.” For the first time in his career, the words felt less like protocol than prayer. One by one, acknowledgments crackled back: different accents, different unit codes, some from formations that should have been decades and parsecs apart. Voss looked toward the battlefield, where the stranded remnants of an empire from many timelines fought to survive in a place none of them understood, and gave the order to advance.
